# Positioning

## Contract

Produce or refresh a company's positioning: the single message the rest of marketing writes from. Output a positioning brief with six parts: the positioning statement, the value proposition, the differentiation (the wedge), the messaging pillars, the message map by audience and funnel stage, and the competitive frame. Ground every claim in real, verified facts; never invent a differentiator or a number. This skill synthesizes into a brief; it produces no finished page.

## Inputs to gather first

Positioning is built from what is true about the company, not invented here. Before writing, get these from the operator (or the company's own source documents):

- Why the company exists and what it actually sells.
- Who the best-fit customer is (the people who buy fastest and stay longest).
- The real edge: the thing the company does that competitors cannot credibly copy, and why.
- Verified proof: outcomes, numbers, names you are allowed to cite. Mark anything unverified as a gap, do not ship it as fact.
- The main alternatives a buyer weighs (including doing nothing).

If a needed fact is missing or unverified, flag it rather than invent it.

## When to use

- Writing or revisiting how the company describes itself (the site, a deck, a one-liner, a bio).
- Before a campaign or a website rewrite, to set the message the copy will carry.
- When copy across channels has drifted and you need to re-anchor to one message.
- Producing the message map a specific audience or funnel stage should use.

## Not for

- Deciding what content to publish or what topics to own.
- Writing the actual page or copy (that is a copywriting task).
- Pricing or offer design (use offer-strategist for the offer).

## How to run

### 1. Pull the facts, then synthesize

Work from the inputs above. Pull the facts; do not restate the documents. Missing or unverified facts become gaps, not guesses.

### 2. Write the positioning statement

One sentence, the classic frame:

> For [target customer] who [need or struggle], [company] is the [category] that [primary benefit], unlike [the alternative], because [the reason to believe / the wedge].

Make [category] a claim the company can own, not a generic label. Make [reason to believe] the wedge or a verified proof, not an adjective.

### 3. State the value proposition

The outcome the buyer gets, in their terms (control, visibility, speed, accountability, fewer fires), not the feature list. Lead with the transformation, name the mechanism second. Keep it specific and free of filler words.

### 4. Name the differentiation (the wedge)

The company's owned position: the one thing it does that a competitor cannot credibly hold, and why. State the reason a competitor cannot simply copy it (a structural advantage, a hard-won capability, an honest tradeoff they will not make). Cite the basis for the claim; do not assert an edge you cannot defend.

### 5. Build 3-5 messaging pillars

Each pillar is a buyer-facing message the company proves repeatedly. Per pillar: the claim, the proof that backs it (verified only), and the objection it answers. Keep one throughline dominant (the wedge); the rest support it. Resist the urge to say five things equally loud.

### 6. Map the message to audience and stage

A short table: for each key audience, and for awareness / consideration / decision, the angle that lands. Different buyers care about different things; do not write one message and assume it fits all of them.

### 7. Frame the competition honestly

The alternatives the buyer weighs. For each, state the company's honest edge: what they do well, and where you are genuinely better for this buyer. No disparagement, no invented competitor claims. Include "doing nothing" as an alternative, because it is usually the real one.

### 8. Output the positioning brief

Deliver the six parts as one brief: positioning statement, value proposition, differentiation, messaging pillars (claim + proof + objection), message map, competitive frame. Mark any unverified proof as a gap to clear before it ships. This brief is the source the website, decks, and campaigns all write from.
