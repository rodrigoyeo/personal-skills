# Negotiation: close without discounting your way there

## Contract

Help the seller negotiate and close on value, not price. This skill gives the tactics and the honest guardrails. It follows one rule: honest-only. The only urgency is the real decision deadline, the walk-away is real and not a bluff, and we never manipulate. It produces a negotiation plan and responses, not a price change.

## When to use

Use after the proposal or offer is on the table and the other side pushes on price, scope, or terms, or to prepare for a close call. Pairs with alex-hormozi: build the value before you defend it.

## Not for

Designing the offer itself (use alex-hormozi). Approving a discount beyond your authority (that is a decision for whoever owns pricing). It produces a negotiation plan and responses, not a price change.

## How to run

Read the situation, pick the play, and keep to the principles. Never negotiate against yourself.

### Principles

1. **Options are the anchor.** Present a small range of options (for example a focused, a full, and an expanded scope) and let the options do the anchoring. Never volunteer a discount and never present a single naked number. If asked for "just a price," give the range.
2. **Trade, never give.** Every concession buys something back: a faster decision, a larger deposit, a case-study or testimonial right, a reference, or reduced scope. A free discount teaches the buyer the price was never real.
3. **Concede on scope, not on method.** To hit a budget, narrow scope (fewer deliverables, a smaller phase, phase it over time). Never cut the parts that make the work actually succeed; those are why you win.
4. **Price objection = a value or scope gap.** "Too expensive" means the value is not yet seen or the scope is bigger than the budget. Reframe to value (the cost of the status quo, the risk of doing nothing) or move to a smaller, still-complete option. Do not just lower the number.
5. **Confirm authority and timeline first.** "Who else signs off?" and "when do you need this done?" before any concession. Concessions to a non-decider are wasted.
6. **Know the walk-away.** A deal is not worth it below your floor, with no internal champion, or when it is a pure price-shopping exercise. Be willing to walk; your scarce capacity is the only honest leverage you have.

### The common plays

- **"Too expensive":** reframe to value and the cost of doing nothing, then offer a smaller-scope option as a complete, focused solution. Not a discount on the same scope.
- **"Can you do better on price?":** "I can shape the scope to fit a budget. Which outcomes matter most right now?" Trade scope for price; never cut price at the same scope.
- **"Competitor X is cheaper":** "What are they not including?" Differentiate on what they leave out; do not reflexively price-match.
- **"I need to think about it":** uncover the real concern (price, authority, timing, trust), answer it, and book the next step with a specific date. Do not leave it open.
- **"Just send me the cheapest one":** present the range and show that the focused option is a complete solution at a smaller scope, not a stripped one.
- **"Give us a discount and we sign today":** a real decision deadline is honest leverage, so trade: a faster signature or a larger deposit can earn a defined concession. Never invent a deadline.

### The close

Summarize the option they chose in their own words, confirm the single next step with a date, restate the payment schedule, and send for signature. One call to action, the real valid-until date, no fake scarcity.

## Guardrails

Set your limits before the call, not during it:

- Know your discount floor and who, if anyone, must approve going below it.
- Decide in advance which concessions you will trade and what each one must buy back.
- The walk-away must be real. A bluff that gets called costs the relationship.

## Gotchas

- Never present a single naked price, never mark one option as "the one to pick," never discount for free.
- The walk-away must be real.
- Match the other side's language and context; keep it human.
